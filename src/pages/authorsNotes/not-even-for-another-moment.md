## Premise

Some time ago—let’s say, thirty five years ago—a planet was attacked by aliens. Now, thirty five years later, on a human world thirty five light years away, plans are underway for a celebration of life, as many of the survivors will make the pilgrimage to watch their planet get destroyed through powerful telescopes.

A displaced family, including a veteran, travel to a planet that’s just far enough away from the family’s home planet to see the light from the the alien invasion that devastated their homeworld. There’s a whole celebration of dignitaries, social media influencers, and other survivors who all want to watch through very powerful telescopes as their planet was destroyed all those light years away.

## Scratch Pad

After the invasion, I don’t think I even saw another Persephone native outside of my immediate family. The survivors of Persephone scattered across the galaxy, and overnight, an entire culture was chopped up and absorbed into a thousand other melting pots. I had a few albums and movies from Persephone that I played for my kids but the generational gap was too large. My children would never really know what it meant to grow up where I grew up.

\---

You could tell the survivors from the well-wishers. Only when faced with the reality of the trauma does a survivor lose her mask. I looked around that room and saw a dozen faces. And just like that, I felt a sense of belonging, despite recognizing absolutely no one.

What a sad group in which to claim membership.

\---

Seffy, my teenaged daughter, made that noise teenagers make when they simply can’t even for another moment, and demanded, “Why are we travelling to the armpit of the galaxy just to watch something that already happened?\!”

“Because it’s important,” I repeated. We’d had the same back and forth for days.

The worst thing was, she was right. Beetle-three was a nothing planet, another cookie-cutter human world. The only thing it had going for it, at that moment, was that it was thirty five lightyears from my homeworld of Persephone.

And it was almost thirty five years since aliens attacked Persephone.

So, like many of the other displaced survivors, I had gathered my children and my aging father and crammed us all into an outrageously overpriced transport and flown halfway across the galaxy to go and watch the fireworks through some very powerful telescopes.

“I wanna see aliens\!” Percy, my youngest, exclaimed.

My father took the bait. “No you don’t\! When you see their tentacles slithering out from a blown bulkhead, you’ll be running for the nearest airlock\!”

All four of us were on repeat.

“Why do you even care?” Seffy grumped directly at me.

The answer was complicated.

\---

I know it was unintentional, but I couldn’t ignore the irony of a mass migration of Persephone natives arriving in one place at one time. His wife Lenova immediately conscripted Percy and me into cooking and decorating.

“We can drive you into town on the big day, so you don’t have to take that awful transit.”

This was the view that I remembered. You thought I fled some alien monsters chasing us down in the dark? The survivors made it off world while the others didn’t. Really, from grandma waking me to leaving the world took all of 18 hours. There was never a monster to point at; just the fear that one day they would realize their mistake and come to collect.

“It’s almost time\!”

## Attempt 2

Seffy, my teenaged daughter, made that noise that teenagers make when they simply can’t even for another moment. “Richard’s family got a swimming pool a couple days ago.” When I asked who Richard was, she ignored me, saying instead, “We’ve got finals next week. Good thing we’re flying out to the middle of nowhere instead.”

I said, “I told you to bring your homework.”

We were crammed together on a transport ship, heading for a memorial day event on a planet called Beetle-3, and it was pretty clear Seffy would rather have been anywhere else. Me too, if I was honest. I’d known about this trip for over a year, now. It had always felt so abstract, like a dentist appointment I schedule a year in advance. A good idea at the time. But then the appointment came up, and I realized I hadn’t been flossing and everyone would know . . .

“Mom\!” she whined.

“I already told you. Beetle-3 is hosting a memorial day event for the survivors, and—”

“I know what’s there. Why are we going?\!”

The answer was complicated.

\---

Since that day thirty-three years ago, I don’t think I’d seen another Persephone native outside of my immediate family. The survivors had scattered across the galaxy. Now, I couldn’t ignore the irony of a mass migration of Persephone natives arriving in one place at one time. The arrival gates were packed full of people with the same eyes, the same noses, the same chins—and everyone was squawking, and panicked, and lost, carrying luggage filled to bursting and corralling crying children. I suspected half of the planet was awash in arrivals. It was an evacuation in reverse.

\---

Seffy tried to “accidentally” leave her luggage at the arrival gate. The robotaxi drove us in circles to rip us off. Our hotel was overbooked. By the time Seffy and I made it to the pavilion the ceremonies had already started, and we had both exhausted our patience for each other.

I shook my head at the sight. “Your grandfather would hate this.”

Seffy muttered, “*I* hate this.”

At least we agreed on something.

The pavilion was a sea of green and yellow decorations, outfits, even face paintings. Every single building flew the Persephone flag. We squeezed through the crowd, past vendors selling vintage Persephone toys, Persephone clothing—even Persephone desserts.

Everyone was trying to cash in on the event. We passed telescopes for sale in front of furniture stores. Scalpers tried to sell us tickets to the memorial day event. Whenever someone recognized my heritage, I had to duck and weave past them or else risk being strong-armed into some purchase.

It was uncanny. Unsettling. Persephone had never looked like this place. Someone had spent five minutes watching archival footage and declared themselves an cultural consultant. The cuisine was the worst. You physically can’t make authentic Persephone food anymore.

“Do you want to explore a little?” I asked.

“I want to go back to the hotel.”

I grabbed her hand and led her down the promenade towards the observatory grounds.

\---

Beetle-3 was nothing special. Another cookie-cutter human world, one of hundreds. It just happened to be about thirty-three light years from my homeworld of Persephone. Now, the rays of light that had started a thirty-three year journey from Persephone to Beetle-3 were about to arrive.

\---

Our passes got us okay seats inside the observatory. None of us were actually going to get to look through the telescope; they had projectors set up like a movie theater—a movie theatre playing a thirty-year-old rerun.

Seffy leaned in close. “So what, we listen to some speeches, watch a thirty second livestream, then everyone claps?”

“No one’s going to clap.”

She glanced around at the crowd. “Looks like they might. There’s merch.”

I took a patient breath. “Seffy—whatever happens, do not clap. Lots of people died. Be serious.”

“I am being serious. We all watched clips during school. Like, the actual footage. Documentaries about humanity’s first encounter with aliens. We’ve all seen it.”

I muttered, “Not like this.”

A voice came over the speakers, thanking the sponsors and reminding everyone to keep the aisles clear during the presentation. The lights dimmed a fraction, not enough to hide the crowd, just enough to turn every face into a pale reflection of the screen. Someone nearby laughed too loudly. Someone else was shushing them. The air felt wrong—too warm, too close—like it had already been breathed too many times.

Suddenly, there it was.

On the screen, in poor focus and contrast, with chromatic aberration tinting the edges red and blue, hung a flat greyish-green circle in front of a greyish background. Persephone. Immediately, people started murmuring. Phones came out, brighter than the video feed from the big scope.

Seffy scoffed. “That’s the best they can do? Might as well play something from the archives instead.”

A better mother would’ve scolded her. A better mother would’ve taught her empathy. But she was right. I’d seen similar footage countless times over the years. I crouched lower in my seat and hugged my knees.

At the top left of the screen, a number counted down. Two hundred seconds. The scientists had calculated to the millisecond when first contact would appear. I grabbed my knees and squeezed.

A man had stepped onto the stage, standing in front of the screen, gesturing with his hands without saying anything. I could barely see him through the silhouetted heads in front of me. Barely a hundred seconds left, and still there were people, screens, projectors, and government bureaucracy blocking my view of the final moments of beauty my home would ever perform.

My hands had started to shake. I pressed them into my knees, hard enough to hurt, but they didn’t stop. The room kept shrinking. Or I was. I couldn’t tell. “I’m fine,” I said, too quickly, though Seffy hadn’t asked. The lights dipped again, and a murmur ran through the crowd—anticipation, excitement—and something in my chest seized, sharp and sudden, like I’d missed a step I didn’t remember taking. It was about to start. It was already starting. I couldn’t— I couldn’t be here for this. I felt the world rumbling below me, felt the booming shakes like they were happening again. My mother was yelling at me to wake up and pack my clothes—

“Mom\!” Seffy yelled at me.

“I’m fine,” I blubbered.

She grabbed my hand and yanked me up and out of my seat. “Jesus. Come on.”

“We’re going to miss it\!” I wailed. “It’s starting\!” They say a parent can summon the strength of a locomotive to save her child from danger. Seffy probably didn’t need that much to haul me out of the auditorium and into the open sky.

The sun had set while we were in the line up. Up above through a sea of tears countless beautiful stars twinkled—one of them a little more violently than the others. 

\---

I leaned back against the wall, feeling drained.

Seffy crouched in front of me, clucking over me like I used to do for her.

“Are you going to be okay if I run to the toilet?”

I nodded. I’d had my little moment. She hurried off down the promenade. Minutes later she came back with a box in her arms. It was one of those bargain telescopes.

“Quickly,” she said, tearing the box open. “I think it’s already started.”

\---

Thirty-three years ago, aliens invaded my homeworld of Persephone. My mother and I barely made it out. Humanity won, eventually. But I never went home.

Seffy and I took turns peering through the tiny eyepiece, watching as impossible distances and cheap optics turned fireballs into fireflies. I sniffled, and wiped the tears from my cheeks. She grabbed my other hand and squeezed. I squeezed back.

“Mom? Are you . . . are you there right now?”

I nodded, thinking back to that day. “I think we were on the far side, actually. Right now your granny is waking me up.”

“What about grandpa?”

I took my turn at the eyepiece. “He’s already in space right now, fighting them off.”

“Excuse me?”

An elderly couple were standing nearby. Their faces were familiar.

“May we take a look?”

## Attempt 3

Seffy, my teenaged daughter, made that noise that teenagers make when they simply can’t even for another moment. We were crammed together on a transport ship, heading for a planet called Beetle-3, and it was pretty clear Seffy would rather have been anywhere else. Me too, if I was honest. I’d known about this trip for over a year, now. It had always felt so abstract. Now everything was moving too fast.

“Mom\!”

“I already told you. They’re hosting a memorial day event for the survivors—”

She groaned. “I know what’s there. Why are we going?\!”

The answer was complicated.

\---

Since that day thirty-three years ago, I don’t think I’d seen another Persephone native outside of my immediate family. Now, I couldn’t ignore the irony of a mass migration of them arriving in one place at one time. The arrival gates were packed full of people with the same eyes, the same noses, the same chins—and everyone was squawking and panicked, carrying luggage filled to bursting and corralling crying children. It was an evacuation in reverse.

\---

By the time Seffy and I made it to the pavilion the ceremonies had already started.

The pavilion was a sea of green and yellow decorations, outfits, even face paintings. Every single building flew the Persephone flag. We squeezed through the crowd, past vendors selling Persephone-branded telescopes and opera-style playbooks. It was disgusting. Persephone had never looked like this. Someone had spent five minutes watching archival footage and declared themselves a cultural consultant. The cuisine was the worst. You physically can’t make authentic Persephone food anymore.

I felt nauseous. This wasn’t the Persephone I wanted my daughter to see.

“Your grandfather would have hated this.”

Seffy muttered, “*I* hate this.”

At least we agreed on something.

\---

Beetle-3 was nothing special. It just happened to be in the right place at the right time. Now, the rays of light that had started their thirty-three year journey from Persephone to Beetle-3 were about to arrive.

\---

Our passes got us okay seats inside the observatory. We wouldn’t actually look through the big telescope; they had projectors set up like a movie theater—a movie theatre playing a thirty-year-old rerun.

Seffy leaned in close. “So what, we watch a three minute livestream, then everyone claps?”

I took a patient breath, trying to still my heartbeat. “Seffy—whatever happens, do not clap. Lots of people died. Be serious.”

“I am being serious. We all watched clips during school. Like, the actual footage. Documentaries about humanity’s first encounter with aliens. We’ve all seen it.”

I muttered, “Not like this.”

A voice came over the speakers, sudden and harsh, thanking the sponsors. I could hear a group of teens talking about the aliens and the war. The observatory’s logo flicked off, like the power had been cut.

Suddenly, there it was. On the screen, in poor focus and contrast. With chromatic aberration tinting the edges red and blue. Shaky from vibrations. But it was Persephone. My home.

Seffy scoffed. “That’s the best they can do? Might as well play something from the archives instead.”

I was too distracted to scold her. Seffy was right—we’d all seen this before, and in higher definition. But this was live. And they had added a countdown at the top. The scientists had calculated to the millisecond when first events would appear.

“I’m fine,” I said, though Seffy hadn’t asked.

My hands started to shake. I pressed them into my knees, hard enough to hurt. The room kept shrinking. Or I was. I couldn’t tell. The lights dipped again, and a murmur ran through the crowd—anticipation, excitement—and something in my chest seized, sharp and sudden, like I’d missed a step I didn’t remember taking. I’d had so long to prepare myself, but now that I was here—

I felt the world rumbling below me, felt the booming shakes like they were happening again. My mother was yelling at me to wake up and pack my clothes—

I couldn’t even—

Seffy was yelling at me. “Mom\!”

“I’m fine,” I blubbered.  “I just need a moment.”

She grabbed my hand and yanked me out of my seat. “Jesus\! Come on.”

“We’re going to miss it\!” I wailed.

They say a parent can summon the strength of a locomotive to save her child from danger. Seffy probably didn’t need that much to haul me out of the auditorium and into the open air.

The sun had set while we were in the line up. Up above through a sea of tears countless stars twinkled—one of them a little more violently than the others. 

\---

Much later, I leaned back against the wall, feeling drained.

Seffy crouched in front of me, shaking her head and clucking over me like I used to do for her. I looked away, like she used to.

She asked if I would be okay if she ran to the toilet. Of course. I’d had my little moment. She hurried off down the promenade. Minutes later she stomped back with a box in her arms. It was one of those bargain telescopes.

“Quickly,” she said, tearing the box open. “I think it’s already started.”

\---

Thirty-three years ago, aliens invaded my homeworld of Persephone. My mother and I barely made it out. Humanity won, eventually. But I never went home.

Seffy and I took turns peering through the tiny eyepiece, watching as impossible distances and cheap optics turned fireballs into fireflies. I sniffled, and wiped the tears from my cheeks. She grabbed my other hand and squeezed. I squeezed back.

“Mom? Are you . . . are you there right now?” she asked.

“Yeah. You can’t see me. I’m on the far side, I think. Right now your granny is waking me up.”

“What about grandpa?”

I took my turn so I wouldn’t have to look at her.

“He’s already in space right now, fighting them off.” I took a deep breath, then added, “I never see him again.”

I heard footsteps approaching us. I thought we were about to be told to leave. Instead, an elderly couple were standing nearby. Other survivors, from their facial features. They looked like they couldn’t even for another moment. The man asked, “May we take a look?”
