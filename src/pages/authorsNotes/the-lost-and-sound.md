# The Lost and Sound

The janitor at a museum discovers that the lost and found box doesn’t hold items, but sounds. Each sound represents a memory that someone has forgotten.

## Notes

### Sound and Memory

* Sound is the most fleeting sense. You can’t hold it, you can’t see it looming or watch it leave. Sounds must change in order to be a sound; otherwise it’s just a tone, an ingredient of something else. Sound and memory both rely on absence.  
* Sounds are unique: no two instruments are tuned identically. Meanwhile, no two people have the same memory of the same event.  
* Sound is relational: it always implies a listener, at some distance and direction.  
* It lets you experience someone else’s private moments. It’s fertile space for a lonely listener who hasn’t their own private moments.  
* Silence isn’t ever silent; a person has their own thoughts and body sounds to fill the void. Silence means noticing other sounds  
* You can muffle sounds with felt and insulation. Does that trap a sound, endlessly bouncing around until it either fades or is released?

## Characters

What makes a good character for sounds and memory?

* People who cannot hear well  
* People with poor memory  
* Someone who cannot hear their own voice, someone who forgot their voice a long time ago

### Sound Warden

A vaguely triangular person wearing a large wool coat that flares wide near their ankles. Face obscured. Practically flies during a chase.

### Janitor

An old man working an easy job to supplement his fixed income. At one point in his youth he was more socially active, attending protests for or against things that mattered to him. He feels shame for his views as a youth but not for his courage. Now he watches the world go by and wonders if he has anything left to offer.

## Scene-Sequels and Short Stories

You can use the scene-sequel pattern regardless of whether the one weird thing is new or not to the main character. Either

* the new thing is introduced as the obstacle or complication; or  
* the weird thing is status quo in this universe, in which case the weird thing does something new

The Scene ends the moment neutrality is lost. The Sequel ends the moment he commits to a new path.

## Attempt 1

At the back of the museum is a forgotten room and in that room is Tom, a forgotten retiree working to supplement his fixed income. Once, Tom was a very vocal activist, but now he’s the janitor, and most of his time is spend at the Lost and Sound desk. It doesn’t see much activity, but that suits Tom just fine.

A figure fills the doorway and squeezes his way inside. The Sound Warden is rather triangle-shaped, wearing a pointed hat and a wide wool coat despite the summer weather. His face is always in silhouette, which makes Tom feel uncomfortable.

Tom says, “Welcome. What have you found for me today?”

The Warden also never speaks. He just hovers his hand over the Lost and Sound desk and opens his fist. Somewhere outside, a beercan opens with a hiss-pop-fizz. The Warden closes his fist.

Tom shrugs and pulls out the box from the back shelf—a rather boring- and empty-looking tupperware container with a snapping lid—and remembers thinking, last time, that the box felt rather full. Rather than opening it and risk spilling out dozens of forgotten moments, he cranks the volume on his hearing aids and uses a stethoscope to listen to the contents.

The Sound Warden turns and glides out of the room, too impatient to see what Tom does with the sound.

Tom frowns. The box is full. And it’s not the first time—over the last few months, the Sound Warden and other staff have been dropping off more and more forgotten sounds, while fewer and fewer guests come looking for their forgotten sounds. There’s nothing for it; he’ll have to dump something to make room.

But he has no way of valuing one sound over another. Is that beercan just another beercan, or is it the can a father shared with his son the last time they went fishing together? Is it more valuable than the creaking of an old lady’s favorite rocking chair? Certainly he can’t get rid of the baby’s giggle or the courageous chanting in front of the Parliament buildings or the humming wife doing the groceries or—

No. Tom’s tired of being complicit in the loss of memories. Today he’s going to reunite a lost sound with its owner.

\---

The upper levels are loud and hard to navigate. Murmurs and whispers, the tinny voices from interactive exhibits, whining kids and loud cellphone talkers turn the halls into a labyrinth, and it’s only Tom’s poor hearing that lets him survive the auditory onslaught. It’s always busy on a holiday.

Tom finds a mother and her son taking a time out together at the back of an exhibit. He approaches carefully—parents are always anxious around strangers—but his employee badge and old age make him as unthreatening as the exhibit.

“Hello. Have you forgotten a sound recently?”

The woman frowns. “Do kids make a certain sound when they trust you? I swear mine used to. Now all I get are arguments. Or maybe that’s me.”

Tom puts a hand on her shoulder, gives her a smile, and moves on.

The bored-looking barista at the gift shop: “The clink of a real teacup on a saucer. Now everything’s disposable. Tch.”

A homeless man: “The sound of a nice crackling fireplace. Used to relax me. Now I only hear the smoke alarm.”

The museum is full of people who’ve lost precious sounds and memories.

As he retreats to a quieter side of the museum he sees a familiar figure stalking the halls. Tom follows for a few minutes, trying to stay out of sight. The Sound Warden glides soundlessly through a crowd of people, hands trailing behind him as he does. The visitors all seem struck with a malaise.

“Hey\!” Tom yells.

The Sound Warden whirls around, sees Tom. For a moment the two stare at each other. Then the Sound Warden bolts, and Tom, a retiree, rushes to follow.

Down a hall, past bewildered and lost visitors, into an exhibit. Panting, Tom whirls around. The Sound Warden is blocking the only way out.

“I used to know your name. Did you steal that, too?” he roars.

The Sound Warden throws open his wool coat. A sludge of faded, distorted memories pours out, burying Tom in white noise: a reader cracking the spine of a new car in the lot and setting off the buzzer at the end of a hockey game in the arena where the em cee announces two heavyweights circling one another like crows and seagulls fighting for dropped fish and wood chips from a roaring chainsaw—

Tom yells for help but no sound comes out, and when he wheezes for breathe no air comes back in. He’s going to drown in the remnants of forgotten sounds, and his dying protests will wind up in the Lost and Sound box—

Of course\!

Lying on his back, with the Sound Warden looming over him, Tom grabs the Lost and Sound, aims it at the Sound Warden, and pops open the lid.

Trumpets. Applause. Laughter. Full-bodied, rich memories from real people blast out like a shell from a cannon.

The Sound Warden’s wool coat swells and stretches, buttons popping off one after another. He’s stuck in the doorway, too wide to escape. With a cry the Sound Warden bursts in a storm of shredded wool.

It’s quiet, blessedly. And Tom is still breathing, though his heart is hammering. An onlooker rushes in, calls for help. Museum staff help him to a bench and then down to the first aid room to recover.

He’s still holding the Lost and Sound, though the lid is missing. Inside is only a single sound: a young man chanting and cheering for causes he once cared about—the sound of a courageous man standing up for something.

## Attempt 2

Every sound you’ve ever lost ends up somewhere.

In a forgotten room at the back of the Museum is a forgotten man named Tom. Tom used to conduct the Victoria Symphony, but after losing his hearing and retiring, he now works part-time at the museum’s help desk.

A figure fills the doorway. The Sound Warden is a strange fellow — all triangles and shadows, wearing a pointed hat, a thick wool coat, and oven mitts. Tom thinks he once knew the fellow’s name, but it’s rude to ask.

“Hello, again, sir. What have you brought me today?”

Wordlessly, the Sound Warden reaches into his coat and opens the palm of his oven mitt to the ceiling. Somewhere outside the room, a beer can opens with a hiss-crack-fizz.

“Interesting.”

The Sound Warden turns and glides out of the room.

Tom retrieves the Lost and Sound box from the back. It feels rather heavy. Rather than risk spilling a tidal wave of discordant sounds everywhere, he pulls out his stethoscope and listens.

It’s just as Tom feared. The Lost and Sound is full, and he’d have to evict something to make room. But what’s worth keeping? Was that a beercan from a father and son’s last fishing trip? Certainly he can’t get rid of the baby’s giggle or the humming wife doing the dishes or—

No. Tom’s lost enough of his own sounds throughout his life, and it’s been years since anyone else has heard him. He won’t just spill them out onto the floor and let them fade away. Today, Tom's going to find one of them a new forever home.

\---

The upper floors have been taken over by interactive exhibits on the history of music. Songs from wildly different eras combine with wailing children and murmured phone calls to create a sonic natural disaster, and Tom is thankful, for once, for the low volume on his hearing aids.

Tom finds a mother and her son taking a time out together at the back of an exhibit.

“Sorry to bother you.” He holds up the Lost and Sound box. “Can I interest you in some crashing waves?”

The mother shakes her head, looking frantic, as her child tries to scramble free. “I used to know what it sounded like to be in control of my life. I don’t need more noise.”

To the security guard standing at the back, ignored and invisible, he offers thunderous applause.

“No thanks. If you’ve got it, I’d take a quiet thank you from someone who means it.”

To a sad looking man, he offers the sound of a city bus pulling away from the sidewalk.

“Urgh. That’s how she left me. If you’ve got it, I’d take the clink of someone starting a speech at a wedding?”

Turns out, no one wants a hand-me-down sound.

\---

As he turns a corner he sees a familiar figure stalking the halls. Tom follows for a few minutes. The Sound Warden glides through the crowd, with his hands and their oven mitts trailing behind him.

And in his wake, the guests are turning grey and muted, struck by some sort of malaise . . . He’s dredging their sounds\!

“Hey\!” Tom calls out.

For a moment the two stare at each other.

Then the Sound Warden bolts. Tom follows. Past bewildered visitors. Down a hall. Into a silent exhibit. Dead end. Panting, Tom whirls around. The Sound Warden is blocking the door, trapping Tom and his sounds with the bulk of his wool coat.

Tom roars, “I used to know your name. Did you steal that too?”

The Sound Warden throws open his wool coat.

Tom’s hearing aids scream a wall of white noise directly into his skull: buzzers and chainsaws and hydraulic presses and screeches and cawing birds and crying children.

Tom falls back, yells for help, but no sound comes out, and when he wheezes for breath no air comes back in. A hammer drives into his chest. He’s going to drown in this sludge of forgotten sounds, and his dying protests will wind up in someone else’s Lost and Sound box—

Of course\!

As the Sound Warden looms over him, Tom grabs the Lost and Sound. Everything in the box will be lost—but if this is his last stand, then he’ll be damned if he dies listening to this modern crap. He points it at the Sound Warden and throws open the lid.

Cymbals and trumpets and banging drums and raving applause erupt from the box, harmonious and clear and beautiful. Classic.

The Sound Warden’s coat billows, swells out and out and out, jamming him stuck in the doorway until he detonates with a bang\!

Tom collapses on his back, panting. Shredded wool floats around him. The sounds of everyday life flood back in. His heart is hammering. An onlooker rushes in, calls for help. A frantic mother and an impotent security guard and a lonely divorcee help him to a bench and then down to the first aid room to recover.

As his heart rate settles, he realizes he’s still clutching something in his hands. The Lost and Sound’s lid is missing, and almost certainly empty. He grabs his stethoscope and probes the inside, just in case, and hears a single, clear sound: the soft tac-tac of a baton on the stand, the breath before the first note.
