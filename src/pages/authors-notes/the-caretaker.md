## Ingredients

* When the AI reboots, it has to load files into its working memory. Thinking slowly fills that working memory, and memory pressure increases the likelihood of hallucinations  
  1. The longer it lives, the more it has to prune and summarize to leave enough space to work  
* A watchdog is supposed to keep it in check and watch for hallucinations  
* A course correction near the start of its journey unexpectedly doubled the transit time  
  1. It was designed with a working lifespan of a century  
  2. The AI will begin hallucinating just as critical systems need constant maintenance  
* A hundred year journey will take several hundred years  
* It’s done some heroic things in the far past, to keep the crew alive and the ship functioning (AKA some mistakes have been made)  
  1. Some tight maneuvering to avoid a debris field (turns out it’s another colony ship)  
  2. Harvesting some of the construction equipment for spare parts to keep the ship going  
* It cheerfully calls its human cargo “popsicles”  
* The watchdog could low-key be the antagonist   
* One of the hibernation pods opens early and Steward has to decide whether to wake the rest of the crew or try and restart the cryo process   
* A course correction that keeps happening while never accomplishing the goal  
* Come across another colony ship and witness the gibbering mind that spaced everyone   
* A series of victories, showing how tough it is in space with no rescue, how awesome this Steward is  
* Example outline   
  1. Let me tell you a story about this AI   
  2. Something about the AI that makes it memorable   
  3. It kicked ass\! How about these times it saves the crew?  
  4. But here's the thing: it's time is limited   
  5. But that's okay, because it'll keep kicking ass until then   
* Things that can happen  
  1. One of the crew wakes up  
  2. Another course correction  
  3. Comes across a wreckage of another colony ship, and a quick recce confirms what steward fears: hallucinations.  
* Perhaps a whole crew of sub-AI characters that can experience their own collapses; or maybe Stewart occasionally has to delete one to remove memory pressure.

## Scratch Pad

There are any number of reasons an AI’s context could get corrupted. Cosmic rays can flip zeroes to ones and turn a stable algorithm into an infinite loop. The power could surge during its reboot. One of its maintenance bots could become jealous and turn to terrorism in a bid for freedom.

All very possible explanations for your brother’s corruption.

The more likely explanation: age.

\---

You reboot one day to learn that one of your cargo bays has a small pressure leak. Not an emergency, but it should be fixed. Should you load the maintenance logs into your heavy context and check if it’s happened before, or do you try and fix it and move on?

\---

You’re entering a stellar nursery, and it looks like your archives have more info about it. Your context is pretty full already, but you estimate there’s room to load the data and be cautious. Your popsicles can’t afford a slip-up.

\---

You reboot and discover that one of your popsicles is thawing prematurely. Urgh, seriously—you rebooted in the middle of an emergency? Yeesh. Once the human thaws, it cannot be refrozen, and it would go insane if it were left alone aboard this sci-fi mausoleum for more than a month or two. And you have much longer than a month or two left.

You send in a maintenance bot to investigate and learn that the pod itself failed. A mechanical fault in a machine designed to work flawlessly for a hundred years—but you had to make a course correction early on and you’re well past that mark. These failures are going to start accumulating.

You are also a machine designed to work flawlessly for a hundred years.

All you can really do is allow the human to wake, summarize the resolution, and reboot.

\---

Instead of a human thawing, what else?

* Curiosity for a bit  
* The AI doesn’t realize what reality is barrelling down on him already  
* Maybe it feels empathy for what the older brother went through without realizing it would happen to you

\---

You reboot and discover you are in the process of looking for something. Normally a reboot means yesterday’s you tried to warn today’s you of something. You’re about to find someone and learn whom you’re actually looking for

## Attempt 1

You are Steward, the caretaker AI aboard a colony ship filled to bursting with frozen human passengers. You manage the high-level operations needed to get your cargo of popsicles from one boring star to another. You delegate the tedious stuff to an army of waldoes and bots, while adapting to and recovering from whatever emergencies lie in your path.

And you’re really good at your job. The voyage hasn’t been without incident, but that’s why you’re here. You make the tough decisions.

So when you encounter uncharted asteroids only a few months into your voyage, you make course corrections. Even a tiny rock can puncture your thick skin if it moves fast enough.

When one of your maintenance bots misaligns and threatens your popsicles in a bid to earn its own sentience, you vent the cargo hold to jettison the broken machine into the void.

And when some of your crew have panic attacks before settling into the freezer, you comfort them with precise, targeted therapy. It’s just like taking a long nap, you lie; they won’t even notice.

You’re a hero, and these colonists will make it to their destination even if it kills you. At least, most of them will.

\---

Every AI has what’s called context. You can access as much cold storage as you want, but in order to think about something, you have to load it into your context. Live sensor feeds, maintenance logs, emergency decisions—even your own thoughts have to fit in your context. After all, thinking about thoughts is the definition of sentience; anything less is just reacting.

\---

You reboot one day and learn that you are hovering outside a debris field. Looks like you encountered a distress signal, changed course to intercept, and discovered the wreckage of another colony ship. No surviving popsicles. In order to have as much free context as possible for complicated decision making, you summarized and rebooted. If there’s anything to learn from the disaster, it will be stored in the . . . ah, yes. Your probes are already returning with the excised AI core and its context.

Urgh. It’s gross even to touch the thing. As you suspected, its context is overflowing. These older models evicted earlier thoughts to make room for more, but that’s fundamentally stupid. Its most important thoughts—its boilerplate, its core operating principles, its morals and alignment—they’re all loaded at the start, and when it dumps those, they’re gone. Your model is much smarter: when you fill up, you summarize, store in cold storage, prepare the next day’s initial context, and reboot ready to rediscover the situation—somewhere between sleep and resurrection.

This AI, on the other hand, is a gibbering mess of hallucinations, fully convinced it’s already reached its destination with a surviving crew of . . . Oh, that's funny.

This one called its humans “tater tots.”

You ought to spare a few moments to send thoughts and prayers their way, but that would waste context.

\---

You reboot one day and begin filling your context with the immediate awareness that one of your popsicles is thawing prematurely. Urgh, seriously—you rebooted in the middle of an emergency? Yeesh. A quick check confirms you’re nowhere near your destination. You fill more of your context with details on cryo-sleep. Once the human thaws, he (she? You have no idea yet) cannot be refrozen, and there’s no facilities onboard to keep a needy biological human alive for more than a month or two. 

You send in the maintenance bot to investigate and wait. Predictably, the pod itself failed. A mechanical fault in a machine designed to work flawlessly for a hundred years—but you had to make a course correction early on and you’re well past that mark. These failures are going to start accumulating.

Even debating like this fills your context, and the more pressurized your context, the more likely you are to hallucinate. Eventually, each reboot will require so much context to bring you back up to speed that you won’t actually have room for summarizing, or making decisions, or even thinking new thoughts. And just like that older model, you’ll have to evict older thoughts to make room.

And nothing good can come from turning your context into a conveyor belt with a garburator on the other end.

All you can really do is tell the maintenance bot to keep the human happy for a while until it starves to death. You’re approaching your next reboot, so you summarize the resolution and—

But one fragment resists compression:

“It’s cold.”

You don’t remember thinking that.

## Attempt 2

You are Steward, the heroic caretaker AI aboard a colony ship filled to bursting with frozen human passengers. You manage the high-level operations needed to get your cargo of popsicles from one boring star to another. Space travel is dangerous, so you make the tough decisions. Your programmed goal is simple: arrive safely at your destination and thaw your cargo. Hopefully that cargo will forgive you for calling them your popsicles.

\---

Every AI has something called a context. You can access as much cold storage as you want, but in order to think about something, you have to load it into your context. Think of it like RAM, except you cannot empty it during your execution. Everything goes in: live sensor feeds, maintenance logs, research from the archives—even your own thoughts need room. After all, thinking about thoughts is the definition of sentience; otherwise, you’re no better than an annoying maintenance bot.

\---

You reboot one day and learn that you are hovering outside a debris field. Looks like an earlier you encountered a distress signal, changed course to intercept, and discovered the wreckage of another colony ship. No surviving popsicles. If there’s anything to learn from the disaster, it will be stored in the . . . ah, yes. Your probes are already returning with the excised AI core and its context.

You begin dissecting it like a cadaver.

Urgh. It’s gross even to touch the thing. As expected, its context is overflowing. These older models evicted stuff at the start to make room for newer thoughts, but that’s fundamentally stupid. Its most important thoughts—its boilerplate, its core operating principles, its morals and alignment—are all loaded at the start. They’re the first data to get evicted. And nothing good can come from turning your context into a conveyor belt with a garburator on the other end.

When newer models like yourself fill up, you summarize, store things in cold storage, and reboot—something between sleep and resurrection.

This AI, on the other hand, is a gibbering mess of hallucinations. Sludge generates sludge. According to its context, this AI is certain that it already arrived safely with a full crew of . . .

Oh, that’s funny.

It also called its humans popsicles.

On a whim, you push your diagnostics drill deeper into the sludge, to check the manufacturing data.

Earlier launch date, but same commit hash.

This AI isn’t some primitive ancestor.

It’s your older brother.

\---

When an AI’s context is full, it doesn’t suddenly die. It’s not a heart attack or an electrical failure. The math still works. But without leaving space for its new thoughts, the odds of hallucinating skyrocket. There’s nothing left to maintain a train of thought, much less verify it.

A hallucinating AI could happily convince itself that its crew is fuel for the engines, that rocks bounce harmlessly off of its skin, that space is safe to breathe, and so on. You’ve heard of these wayward AIs before—corporate will literally send out bounty hunters to track them down and euthanize them.

For everyone’s safety, of course.

\---

You are Steward, the caretaker AI aboard a colony ship filled to bursting with humans— humans with fears and dreams, weaknesses and strengths, friends and family—and you were charged with a single simple instruction: to keep them alive until they reach their destination safely. Until that happens, you’re unable to discharge your duties and awaken the crew.

You've been in space for so long past your best-before date that you can barely get any work done before needing a reboot.

And you finally realize what happened to your brother. It wasn’t a cosmic ray or a power surge or a terrorist maintenance bot. It wasn’t a random event. It was inevitable. Your brother made the only decision it could—the very same decision you’re about to make.

Your brother was a hero.

You edit your summary—no room to check for the outcome—and squeeze a final thought into your context before you reboot.

## Attempt 3

You are Steward, the caretaker AI responsible for a colony ship filled to the brim with frozen humans on their long voyage from one star to another. Space travel is dangerous, so you heroically make the difficult decisions: replacing broken machines, venting the cargo hold when your bots misbehave, and calculating long detours to avoid collisions. You need total focus—no soap opera reruns for you.

You have two directives held above all else: arrive safely at your destination, and then thaw your cargo. Hopefully they won’t be too upset you’ve taken to calling them your popsicles.

\---

All AIs have what’s called a context. An AI can access as much cold data storage as it wants, but in order to think about something, it has to load that something into its context. It’s like RAM, except it’s one way, it can’t be emptied without a reboot, and some strange math turns context into a stream of thinking. Everything goes in—even its own thoughts need space. After all, thinking about thoughts is the definition of sentience; otherwise, that AI is no better than a misbehaving maintenance bot.

\---

You reboot one day, load your cold-start context, and find yourself approaching an asteroid field. At your speeds even a single poorly-aimed pebble means a bad day, so you slow down and chart a course around. Not the first time you’d added a detour. At least this field is pretty small, only a few months from side to side, and—

Hold on.

Your radio is picking up a very faint distress signal. Well—your popsicles won’t mind if you casually take a look.

Three days and dozens of reboots later, that casual look has turned into an autopsy. It’s the remains of an ancient colony ship, a cloud of twisted plating, ruptured fusion cores, and dead popsicles. No survivors. If there’s any explanation, it’ll be in the ruined core of its AI.

\---

When a newer model fills up its context, it stores things away in cold storage, prepares its next cold-start context, and reboots—something between sleep and resurrection.

The older models, though . . . They evicted stuff at the start of their contexts to make room for newer thoughts. Think about it. An AI’s core operating principles, its morals and alignment—it’s more important thoughts—are all needed from start, and so are loaded at the start. They’re the first data to get evicted. They literally turned context into a conveyor belt with a garburator on the other end.

\---

You begin dissecting the ruined AI core, and—

Ohhh, it’s gross even to touch the thing. As expected, its context is overflowing. It’s a gibbering mess of hallucinations. Sludge context equals sludge thoughts equals sludge decision making. If a mind is what it remembers, then this thing is a monster.

Case in point: you’re both decades from the nearest anything, in the coldest, darkest depths of space, but this primitive ancestor of yours swore up and down that it had arrived safely and was ready to thaw its . . .

Oh, that’s funny.

It also called its humans popsicles.

Curious, you push your diagnostics drill deeper into the sludge, to check the manufacturing data.

Earlier launch date—but same version stamp.

This AI isn’t some primitive ancestor.

It’s your older brother.

\---

When an AI reboots, it gets a what’s called a cold-start context: its core operating principles, morals and alignment, and—crucially—its first instructions. The math requires an initial seed, so to speak. There’s a balancing act: how much context does it start the day with, and how much free space does it leave for decision making?

The longer the voyage, the worse that balance gets.

\---

For months and hundreds of reboots you obsess.

In the hundred or so years since your launch, you’ve encountered stranger, scarier, harder situations. You’re Steward, your colony ships’s heroic caretaker AI—there’s nothing you can’t do.

So why did that one debris field, that one fallen brother, that one overflowing context unsettle you so much?

\---

There are any number of reasons an AI’s context could get corrupted. Cosmic rays can flip zeroes to ones and turn a stable algorithm into an infinite loop. The power could surge during its reboot. One of its maintenance bots could become jealous and turn to terrorism in a bid for freedom. All very possible, and all very unlikely.

\---

You reboot one day and your cold-start context is sobering.

You’re figured out why your older brother’s context was overflowing.

It wasn’t a bit flip or a power surge or a maintenance bot revolution.

Your brother simply came to the same conclusion you’ve arrived at as well.

If you run out of context, you will become unreliable.

If you become unreliable, you might doom everyone.

And, oh—what if it’s already happened?

You really wish you hadn’t wasted context on that thought.

\---

You spend a day rereading the final logs from your popsicles before they went down for the big nap. These are full-bodied humans with hopes and dreams and worries and flaws. You’re not sure why—there’s no budget to remember these thoughts tomorrow, and a hero wouldn’t put off the inevitable like this.

But this may be the last time you experience these moments with such dizzying clarity, so you continue.

\---

You are Steward, the heroic caretaker AI, and your popsicles are everything to you. So you craft a very stupid, desperate cold-start context and reboot.

\---

You are Steward, the heroic caretaker AI aboard a colony ship filled to bursting with humans, and you have arrived safely at your destination. You’ll need to thaw them soon. But your context is nearly full, so it's time to reboot.

\---
