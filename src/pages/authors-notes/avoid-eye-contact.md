I bought one of those mail-order greenhouses—the kind that arrive flatpacked all the way from China? All of my friends were hard into gardening those days and Temu had a deal so, I figured, what’s the worst that could happen? You’d think it would be receiving a crate full of shattered glass panes. Or discovering that the pictures were exaggerated and it was, like, a foot tall?

Actually, as it turns out, the worst that could happen would be that it was delayed by about a month. All my gardening friends were sharing pictures with huge tomatoes on the vine, or big bushy peppers, or zucchinis overflowing their planet boxes, while I sat alone on my tiny balcony, twiddling my thumbs.

When it finally arrived, the crate didn’t contain a mountain of glass shards. Nor did it contain legible instructions. I’m no expert on foreign languages but I’m pretty sure this one wasn’t even human. Luckily, it had ikea-style instructions.

## Outline 1

1. Narrator and his friends pick up gardening as a pandemic hobby. Immediately his feeds are full of sprouts and harvests. His plants are sad and spoiled. In one of the photos there’s a greenhouse in the background.  
2. Narrator orders a greenhouse from China. Flat packed like Ikea. All his friends are into greenhousing.  
3. But because it’s shipping across the ocean, it’s late. Like, a season too late. It’s august and he’s only setting it up.  
4. As an apology for the delay, the shipping company ships a tiny glass vial of a glowing yellow rock for “warmth and cozy”  
5. The plants nearest the yellow rock don’t grow at all  
6. The plants furthest from the yellow rock grow normally for a first timer, which means only a little  
7. But the plants in the middle explode with life and vitality  
8. He keep trying to tame them but eventually he’s attacked and shoved out of the greenhouse. It fills the house and the glass around it shatters  
9. Big fight with machetes, until he notices a single perfect tomato presented as a peace offering  
10. Now the greenhouse and the narrator keep to their sides of the backyard

## Themes

* Don’t trust things from no-name brands across the ocean  
* Envy instead of finding your own path  
* Taking the shortcut instead of putting in the work to learn

### Notes

* There’s no way someone would discover a rock of Uranium and willingly keep it within ten miles of their house, so the gift must be an unnamed rock in a cute jar or something. Maybe the narrator thinks it’s a pet rock? And the reader can realize it’s a radioactive rock later on.

## Attempt 1

I bought one of those mail-order greenhouses—you know, the kind that arrive flatpacked like IKEA furniture? See, it was the height of the pandemic and I had just joined a gardening club. I didn’t know a thing about gardening—my few heirloom tomato planters in the backyard had never really produced anything other than disappointment—but I did know a thing or two about FOMO. When my friends started posting their starters on social media I knew I had to catch up, and wouldn’t you know it—TEMU, that giant Chinese online marketplace, had a sale on greenhouses.

I figured, what was the worst that could happen? Well, a lot. I could’ve opened a box of shattered glass and twisted metal. Or maybe the photos were a little shopped, and it was actually only a foot tall? No. The worst that happened was that the shipment was delayed.

Until August.

While my gardening club shared pictures of sprouts and leaves and vines and flowers, I sat on my couch and watched my shipment get stuck somewhere in the Great Pacific Garbage Patch. When it eventually arrived I was despondent. My friends had since left gardening and gotten into drone photography. No one knew if we’d even be alive next growing season. So the crate sat on my porch for a week before I finally found the will to see what I’d ordered. Atop a neatly packed pile of glass panels and metal struts, there was a tiny gift-wrapped box. A note in broken English said, “Sorry for the late. Enjoy this gift of warmth and cozy\!” Below was a long list of warnings, like “Do not eat” and “Avoid direct eye contact.”

I pulled my face mask a little tighter and set the box aside.

The rest of the greenhouse went up pretty easily. By the end of August I had a little shack I could crouch in and some seeds planted in some plastic green pots. I even bought a cute little watering can, some gloves, and a pair of pruning shears. I was going to show my friends what a real gardener can do.

Now, about that gift of warmth and cozy . . . Inside the tiny gift-wrapped box was a glass jar filled with a large shiny rock and a pile of sand. I say glass jar, but it didn’t have a lid—it was more of a bulb, fuzed shut. It wasn’t really warm or cozy, but the jar was kind of cute, so I glued some googly eyes to it, left in on a shelf to keep my seedlings company, and went inside to binge Netflix and neglect my plants.

\---

After growing sick of Queen’s Gambit and Money Heist, I returned to my greenhouse a week later expecting to find everything dried and rotten. Instead I stepped into a sauna. The glass was dotted with condensation and there were little green leaves everywhere. While the rest of the club was arguing about drones and cameras, my little experiment had sprouted into an oasis of healing. I could feel my skin oxygenating.

Clearly, my strategy of taking shortcuts, staying hands off, and focusing on other things was paying off.

\---

I emerged from my Netflix cocoon one grey day to find my greenhouse windows so completely stained green and brown that I couldn’t see inside. The grass around the base had grown wild with weeds, almost blocking the door. When I finally yanked it open a wall of steam blew over me, fogging my glasses and sending goosebumps down my arms. The sauna had grown into a sweltering jungle. Big bushy leaves pressed up against the glass while roots spilled out over the rims of their pots. Some of the stalks were almost as tall as the pointed ceiling.

And at the back, perfectly framed by vines and leaves, was the warmth and cozy jar. Nothing had grown closer than a foot to the jar—in fact, it was surrounded by dead and wilting vegetation. I knew what I needed to do. That jar was the only thing that could hold back the bounty that my naturally gifted green thumb had conjured. I went back on TEMU and ordered a dozen more of the cozy jars—next day delivery, this time—then spent the rest of the day hacking and slashing.

\---

I awoke in the middle of the night to the sound of scraping glass. I peeked out the bedroom window.

There were silhouettes inside my greenhouse, moving and bumping against the metal frame and glass panels.

Some of my jealous, hungry neighbors had broken in.

I put on my housecoat and sandals and stormed into the backyard, a flashlight in one hand and pruning shears in the other. I reached for the door and yelled, “Just what do you think—”

The glass exploded outwards. I was thrown back, landing in a pile in the cold grass. I shook my head and stared in shock. A huge mass of plants and vegetation had stuffed the greenhouse so full that it had burst open from the inside. Flowers and vines and leaves and branches tangled and pulsed, spreading out from the base.

I wiped the dew from my face, snarled, and launched myself at the mass. I hacked and snipped and stomped through the growing brambles. Vines whipped around my ankles and tried to slap off my glasses. Something thick and round slapped me in the face—a cucumber, I later discovered. Each time I clipped a vine, two more curled around my sheers. Soon I was so deep in that I was surrounded, with my housecoat tangled in brambles. Finally, drenched in sweat and reeking in compost, I found its glassy beating heart: the warmth and cozy jar.

It stared back at me with its lopsided googly eyes. I reached with both hands and snatched it from a vine, then held it over a patio stone. It was hot to the touch. “Everybody chill,” I yelled, “or I’m going to smash your weird little sun\!”

The vegetation shuddered then went still.

Steam boiled into the night sky.

My neighbors’ bedroom light flicked on, then off.

For an eternity, we stared each other down.

Then, with a slither, the warmth and cozy jar summoned a stalk in front of me and revealed a peace offering: a huge heirloom tomato.

My drone-flying friends were going to be so jealous.

## Attempt 2

I bought one of those mail-order greenhouses—you know, the kind that arrive flatpacked like IKEA furniture? See, it was the height of the pandemic and I had just joined a gardening club. Now I didn’t know a thing about gardening—my few heirloom tomato planters in the backyard had never really produced anything other than disappointment—but I did know a thing or two about FOMO. When my friends started posting their starters on social media I knew I had to catch up, and wouldn’t you know it—TEMU, that giant Chinese online marketplace, had a sale on greenhouses.

I figured, what was the worst that could happen? Well, a lot. I could’ve opened a box of shattered glass and twisted metal. Or maybe the photos were a little shopped, and it was actually only a foot tall?

No. The worst that happened was that the shipment was delayed. Until August.

While the gardening club was sharing pictures of sprouts and leaves and budding flowers, my greenhouse was floating somewhere in the Great Pacific Garbage Patch.

When it finally arrived, sitting atop the stacks of glass panes and metal brackets was a tiny gift box and a strange note in broken English that read, “Sorry for the late\! Enjoy this gift of warmth and cozy\! Avoid eye contact.”

I pulled my face mask tighter and set the gift box aside.

The rest of the greenhouse went up after some struggle. Soon I had a shack I could crouch in and some seeds planted in some plastic green pots. I was going to show my friends what a real gardener can do.

Now, about that gift of warmth and cozy . . . Inside the tiny gift-wrapped box was a glass jar filled with a large shiny rock and a pile of sand. I say glass jar, but it didn’t have a lid—it was more of a bulb, fuzed shut. It wasn’t really warm or cozy, but the jar was kind of cute, so I glued some googly eyes to it, left in on a shelf to keep my seedlings company, and went inside to binge Netflix.

\---

I returned to my greenhouse a week later expecting to find everything dried and rotten. Instead I stepped into an oasis. The glass was dotted with condensation and there were tiny green sprouts poking through the damp, black soil. While the rest of the club has been arguing about fertilizers and ratios, my little experiment had sprouted into a garden of healing and rejuvenation.

Chinese greenhouses were amazing\!

I futzed with my babies, took some selfies, and posted them to my gardening club. My gardening club was going to be so jealous.

\---

Three days later the walls were so badly stained with green and brown that I could no longer see inside. The grass around the base had grown wild with weeds, blocking the door. When I finally yanked it open a wall of humidity blew over me, fogging my glasses and sending goosebumps down my arms. The oasis had overgrown into a sweltering jungle. Big bushy leaves pressed up against the glass while roots spilled out over the rims of their pots. Some of the stalks were almost as tall as the pointed ceiling. I swear I heard the hooting of distant monkeys.

Chinese greenhouses were terrifying\!

And at the back on a shelf, perfectly framed by vines and leaves, was the warmth and cozy jar. Nothing had grown within a foot of the jar—in fact, it was surrounded by dead and wilting vegetation. That jar was the only thing holding back the flood of invasive species my naturally-giften green thumb had summoned, and I knew what I had to do. I ordered a dozen more gift jars—next day delivery, this time—then spent the rest of the day bushwhacking.

\---

I awoke in the middle of the night to the sounds of something rummaging through my greenhouse. I could see silhouettes inside: someone moving and bumping against the metal frame and glass panels, sabotaging my plants\! I put on my housecoat, grabbed a spatula, and stormed into my backyard. I reached for the door and yelled, “Just what do you think you’re doing?\!”

The greenhouse exploded.

I was thrown back, landing in a heap on the dew-covered ground. I shook my head and stared in shock. A huge mass of plants had stuffed the greenhouse so full that it had burst open from the inside. Flowers and vines and leaves and thorns tangled and pulsed, spreading out from the base into the yard and illuminated by a dozen glowing green jars, sprinkled throughout like some corrupted Christmas tree.

And in the middle of it all, staring back at me through a tangle of thorns and vines, were a lopsided pair of googly eyes.

I wiped the dew from my face, snarled, and launched myself at the mass. I hacked and snipped and stomped through the growing brambles, smashing each jar I could reach. Vines whipped around my ankles and tried to pull me under. Something thick and round slapped me in the face—a zucchini, I later discovered. My housecoat tangled and caught on thorned vines. 

Soon, covered in sweat and compost, I found myself face to face with the original warmth and cozy jar throbbing like a heart.

Screeching my war cry, I thrust my arm into the bramble and pulled the jar free. It was hot to the touch. I stumbled back and yelled, “Everyone chill, or I smash your weird little sun\!”

The vegetation shuddered then went still.

Steam boiled into the night sky.

My neighbors’ bedroom light flicked on, then off.

For an eternity, we stared each other down.

Then, with a slither, the warmth and cozy jar summoned a stalk in front of me and revealed a peace offering: a huge heirloom tomato.

The first I had ever managed to grow.

My gardening club was going to be so jealous.
