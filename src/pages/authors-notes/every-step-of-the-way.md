## Attempt 1

Boz the Wanderer is deep in his head when he walks face-first into the knotted trunk of a pine tree.

He scowls at the tree, then frowns. A pine tree isn’t noteworthy, unless you’re on a world where there aren’t supposed to be pine trees. When he looks around he decides the sky is paler than it was this morning, and the dirt path behind him isn’t familiar. And maybe it’s a few degrees cooler.

One of the puppies that follow him constantly bites at his ankle.

“Alright\! You don’t have to run me into a tree. A simple word or two would suffice.”

Boz kicks at it, but the puppies run off, barking and roughhousing. He hurries to keep up—they’re get even more bitey if he lags behind—and digs around his backpack. His homemade map is crowded and overflowing, but still legible. He thinks he knows what world he’s on.

What he doesn’t know is why.

\---

As Boz pushes forward through the brush he hears the familiar barking of whelps. Many, many whelps. It sounds like he’s found the why. And . . . yes, behind the barking is someone scared and whimpering. At the center of a small clearing is a tall tree, and midway up the tree a young boy hangs precariously off of the ground, his arms around an alarmingly bent branch and his toes barely reaching another. A dozen or so whelps are scrambling around the base of the tree, circling like hungry sharks and barking incessantly.

Boz sighs. The paths probably won’t let him leave, now that he’s here.

“Help\! Help\!” the boy calls, as soon as he sees Boz.

Boz sets down his gear, kicks away a few of the beasts, and steps up to the trunk. “What are you doing up there, lad?”

“They chased me\!”

“Can you climb down? They won’t hurt you, I promise\!”

The branch supporting the boy’s feet snaps and he drops a half foot, barely catching himself. He screams.

Boz throws his gear on the ground and runs towards the trunk. “Okay, lad, I’m coming. Hold on\!”

“Hurry\! I’m—I’m slipping\!”

Boz grabs at the trunk and tries to lift himself up to rescue the boy, but he’s not blessed with height or strength. Would the whelps lead him here just to watch the poor boy fall to his death? Not likely, but not impossible. “Hold on tight\! Can you reach another branch?”

“I—I think so,” the boy pants. He twists and stretches his feet out, trying to shift his weight. “Yeah—yeah\! I think I got—”

His branch snaps. The boy shrieks and falls, holding onto his branch with a death grip while managing to hit damned near every other branch. The tree bounces him outwards and he lands with a sickening crunch on the hard dirt.

The whelps stop barking.

It’s silent.

Boz scrambles over to the boy. The boy’s still breathing. That’s a start.

“Are you alright, lad?”

The boy looks at him, screws up his face, and starts balling.

\---

Boz isn’t the only wanderer. The paths are infamous, connecting and disconnecting worlds apparently at random. Every year scores of the brave and foolish fall prey to the romantic notion of hiking among the stars. Most return starving and dehydrated. Most of the rest don’t return at all. Boz often comes across the remains of would-be wayfarers on his travels. He’s one of the unlucky few who actually wander. It’s a curse, not a blessing. He doesn’t exactly get to decide where they lead him, and when he tries to stay put he winds up even farther away.

The secret is the whelps, the little bastards endlessly gnawing at his ankles and crawling over him. They’re the winds behind a sailboat with no rudder, the manifestation of the simple-minded will of the paths themselves.

A will that isn’t limited to a destination.

\---

“There you go. Careful. Eat tiny bites, and don’t rush.”

The boy tears into Boz’s food with a ravenous hunger. Soon it’s all gone. Impressive, for a tiny boy with one working hand.

Boz shakes his head. “What’s your name, lad?”

The boy lets out a loud belch, then wipes his face and says, “Liam, sir.”

“What are you doing out here, Liam?”

“They chased me\! Those monsters.” He pauses to swallow another mouthful. “I’ve been up there for days, at least\!”

“Days?” Boz scowls and looks in the direction of the fleeing whelps. The paths led him to meet this boy moments before he falls? After being stranded for days? That’s no coincidence.

“Where are you from?”

Liam tells him.

“Never heard of that world.”

“It’s—” The boy shakes his head. “It’s the town . . . ” He raises his good hand and tentatively points back where Boz came from. “Uh . . . It’s not that far. I didn’t run that far. We’re just on the outskirts.”

“Lad, you have no idea.”

“But—Where is this, then?”

Boz tells him.

The boy sputters, “But that’s a whole other world\! You’re—you’re lying\!”

“Have you heard of the paths?”

Liam’s eyes go wide. “No way . . . ”

Boz nods. “Sorry kid. You’re nowhere near home anymore.”

Liam’s lips tremble. “I don’t wanna die\!”

“You’re not gonna—”

Liam sniffles. “Mom and dad said anyone who gets lost on the paths winds up dead. They told me never to go on them, and to not follow strangers, and to stay close to home, and—They’re gonna be so mad\!”

“Wait—”

Liam starts wailing again.

“Great,” Boz mutters to himself. “Just great.”

\---

By the time Liam settles down it’s late afternoon. Boz is anxious to get moving again. The paths aren’t dangerous, necessarily, but he doesn’t like wandering after dark, and the longer they sit the later it’ll be before he finds somewhere to settle for the night.

He sits forward and crouches, starts gathering his things. “How’s your hand? Are you okay to try walking, Liam?”

Liam nods sullenly, and climbs to his feet. He looks ready to say something when they hear a bark from close by. The boy tenses, his eyes growing wide, and looks past Boz. Two of the whelps are standing right behind Boz, nipping at his heels. One lands on his back and claws its way up to his shoulders.

“Yes, yes, I know,” Boz complains, kicking at the whelps. He can feel that tug in his gut, can feel the wind pushing him in a different different direction. “Come on, Liam. Unless you want to stay here.”

Liam grumps, and clings to Boz’s coat. “Where are we going? Are you taking me home?”

Boz weighs his options. “Yes, I’ll take you home. But you’re farther from home than you think. It might take a while. Is that okay?”

Liam shrugs. “Guess so.”

“Good.”

Boz pulls out his map, double checks where he thinks they’re going. Even if Boz is at their whim, it’s still worth it to try and figure it out for himself. That way he can tell if something goes wrong.

Boz lowers the map for Liam to see and points. “See? That’s where we are. And that one, there? That’s where we’re going. I think.”

“You think?”

“It’s not an exact science, lad. The paths aren’t like roads. They go where they want. Like it or not, the whelps chose you. You’re a wanderer now. So we go where they want, too.”

“Okay . . . ”

“But\! I promise you. We’ll eventually get you home. And depending how things work out, your parents might not even notice how long it’s been.” He shoves the map back in his pack and sees how empty it is, now, without any food. “Also we’ll need to get some supplies. How does that sound?”

Liam nods and lets go of Boz’s coat. Boz smiles, tussles his hair, and follows the whelps. It will at least be nice having someone to talk to—

“I gotta go to the bathroom.”

## Attempt 2

Boz the wanderer only realized he’d travelled to another world when he walked face-first into the knotted trunk of a pine tree.

He scowled at the tree, but it was his own fault. His subconscious mind had been trying to warn him for several minutes. The sky was paler and it felt a little cooler. The sun was larger in the sky. When he glanced ahead between the trees, he could see the landscape had suddenly become mountainous. And behind him—the world he was leaving had no pine trees at all.

He heard the growling and barking of a trio of annoying whelps.

“What?” he snapped.

One of them bit his ankle.

“Alright, alright\!” he growled, kicking it, but the puppies ran off down the trail, barking and roughhousing.

What was he thinking about, before he had been so rudely interrupted? It didn’t really matter—lost, like the worlds behind him. By now, if he turned around, he wouldn’t return. The trail obviously wanted him here, so it wouldn’t leave the backdoor unlocked.

\---

The pine woods thinned. Boz followed the hiking trail to emerge onto a hillside. He always paused to take in the first real view of another world. The ground fell away to a lush valley of dark green pine trees. At the very bottom of the valley, a bright blue river traced out a ruler-straight line all the way from one horizon to the other. On the other side were more mountains topped with dull grey peaks that curled up and over like pointy scoops of ice cream.

Boz found a stump near the edge and sat with an exhale. Off came his boots; he wiggled his toes in the air and rested his sore feet. A few puppies wandered up beside him, tongues panting, tails wagging.

Deep in his backpack he found an old granola stick, some dog treats to distract the whelps, and a windbreaker—borrowed from some poor schmuck a couple dozen worlds back who certainly didn’t need it anymore—then fished out his map and started guessing where he was.

\---

The trail led downhill, mercifully. As he walked he hummed a tune he couldn’t place and thought back to a few days ago. The trail rarely led him to other people—thankfully—but every now and then it dropped him right in the middle of a settlement, or a village, or even a campsite. Just like it did four worlds ago: friendly hosts, good food, and very generous hospitality. It never let him linger very long.

The trail wasn’t always obvious. Sometimes it was an official-looking trail through a national park; other times the trail disappeared completely, and he’d spend days finding it again. Sometimes it was buried under inches of snow. Today the trail was a nice middle ground: a deer path, or whatever this world had instead of deer.

And today it decided to fork in two. Both directions led downhill, but they clearly went in opposite ways. Boz barely paused before continuing down towards the right. It felt correct.

“Oh\!” he exclaimed. Of course. The village mother had hummed that same tune.

\---

The whelps started barking somewhere up ahead. Was it too much to hope for some wild game stuck in a tree, waiting for him? He approached as quietly as he could. The trail was just as likely to wander him right into this world’s version of a cougar.

Then he heard—

“Get away\! Stupid—”

At the center of a small clearing was a tall tree, and near the top a young boy hung precariously, his arms around an alarmingly bent branch and his toes barely reaching another. A dozen or so whelps scrambled around the base of the tree, circling like hungry sharks and barking incessantly.

“What are you doing up there?\!” Boz hollered. The boy was way, way up that tree.

“I—I wanted to climb this tree, but then these mongrels distracted me and I slipped. I’m actually a very good climber.”

“Uh huh. How long have you been up there?”

“A couple days.”

“You must be hungry.”

“I—”

The branch under the boy’s feet cracked. He slipped, screamed, and barely caught himself. Now he was really dangling.

“I—Oh, shoot—I guess. A little. Do you have any food?”

“Lad—”

“I’m not coming down unless you share\!”

Another crack. The branch broke between the boy’s clenched fists, leaving him dangling from one hand.

“I’m not stuck\!” he yelled, panic in his voice. “I chose to climb up here\!”

Boz kicked at the whelps circling the tree, trying to shoo them away. “Boy, stop playing and climb down\!”

“I can\!”

Boz smeared his palm over his face, then tossed his backpack on the ground and reached for the lowest branch. “Hold still, I’m coming for you—”

He heard a yelp, several branches snapping, and a dull thud a few feet behind him.

Then silence. Even the whelps shut up.

Boz crouched over the him.

The boy stared up at him, eyes wide, then took a deep gasping breath—

And started wailing.

\---

“Easy. Slow down—don’t eat it too quickly\!”

The boy devoured all of Boz’s food in minutes.

“What’s your name?”

“Liam.” A big gulp of water. “You can call me Liam.”

“What are you doing out here?”

“Those mongrels chased me.”

Boz eyed the pack of whelps off to the side, roughhousing.

Through his travels, Boz watched whelps come and go. The trail seems to recycle them: on some worlds they’re comfortable and familiar like old friends; on others, they’re fresh faced and easily startled.

Today, Boz thinks he recognizes about half of them.

“Boy—”

“Liam.”

“—how long did you say you’ve been out here?”

“Overnight. I, uh, I thought it would be fun to camp in that tree\! I didn’t even notice all of the barking.”

Oh, lords.

Boz has never met another wanderer alive before.

“Are you going to take me home? I don’t want my parents to worry. We can’t be that far.”

And the boy doesn’t even know it.

One of the whelps barked at them. The rest had wandered off down the trail.

Of all the vindictive, petty, passive aggressive trail nonsense—

Boz laughs. “Liam, you have no idea.”
