## Intro

Setting: Cold war-era Soviet Russia, or thereabouts

Characters: A bored KGB agent assigned to tail a nobody scientist.

My current idea is that the scientist lives such a boring life that KGB guy feels like he's in a career-ending position, until at the very end the scientist turns out to be very, very important when some sciency happens.

## Attempt 1

When Agent Shelbourne accepted his promotion, his comrades failed to mention that radios don’t work around the radio observatory, due to some quirk of physics. His western counterparts at the CIA probably have radios that would work; instead, Shelbourne makes do with his printer—a handheld device that really ought not to work except, he supposes, for some other quirk of physics. Short messages direct from the KGB pop out with a buzz and a snip, guiding Shelbourne’s investigation. It doesn’t let him reply, or call for help; it also never seems to run out of paper.

The man he’s tailing is named Hillside, a short, balding man that works as a scientist at the observatory. He is a man of routine, with no social life or ambition outside of whatever work he does. Every work day, Hillside leaves his tiny State-appointed apartment at 6:35 and travels by public transit to the campus where he arrives just before 7\. He does . . . science, Shelbourne supposes, before leaving just after 6 to return home. On the weekends, when he isn’t running errands or visiting the shops, Hillside likes to sit in the park or tinker in his living room.

What a waste of a career. For both of them: Shelbourne could be escorting diplomats or uncovering espionage against the State; instead he stalks this sad lonely man like a pervert, watching him from his car and worrying about him being late for dinner. He hasn’t even been issued a gun.

It’s 8:45, and Shelbourne hasn’t left the campus yet. The lights in his lab are still on. Some unexpected lab result? An accident?

His printer buzzes and snips a tiny message into his palm that says, “Investigate.”

\---

Shelbourne’s badge grants him access to the campus, though he hates using it. The badge seems to emit a field of skepticism and resentment, and so wherever he goes, he’s surrounded by unpleasant and reluctantly helpful people.

Still, it shuts up the security guard that hassles him.

The hall smells of cleaning solution. His footsteps echo into the darkness. Few lights are still on this late. Pictures of balding, overweight Party-appointees watch Shelbourne as he navigates the maze of corridors. Hillside’s lab, when Shelbourne finally reaches it, is empty, even though the lights are still on. The dining hall is closed, with only a pair of janitorial staff inside. The local party official has no idea who Hillside is, the useless bureaucrat. In fact, no one knows where he is.

A small thrill sparks to life in Shelbourne’s chest. An anomaly. Odds are Hillside simply left with a colleague or felt ill during the day. While Shelbourne was wandering the campus, Hillside was probably returning home, and is probably smoking and listening to the radio. Odds are Shelbourne’s going to get in trouble for this.

Only—Hillside never leaves early, and he has no friends.

Shelbourne glances around, and looks out the window at the campus.

There’s only one other place Hillside could be.

But, according to Shelbourne’s briefing, Hillside shouldn’t have access to the observatory itself.

Shelbourne stands in front of the radio observatory at the end of the campus. It doesn’t look like any observatory Shelbourne has ever seen—no telescope, no dome, no retractable weather shield. It’s almost certainly a satellite dish, like the kind dotting the roof of the local party headquarters, although much, much larger. Red lights blink around the rim, and atop the receiver. The lights are off inside, and the doors are locked, and Shelbourne doesn’t fancy breaking in—too much paperwork.

His printer buzzes and snips a tiny message into his palm—a string of digits that apparently unlock the door in the loading bay. Shelbourne glances around while the big, heavy door swings open, looking for the camera that must be watching him.

It’s dark inside, this late in the evening. Overhead he can hear the clanking of a hot radiator in a cool room. His skin tingles, and he smells iron. As he explores, he passes warning after warning, heading deeper into the observatory. At the end of the hallway is a stairway leading downwards. He peers over the railing—the shaft descends into the darkness.

There’s no way Hillside is down here—even if he was some super important scientist, all of the science should be up near the dish. Right?

But someone at headquarters wants him here.

The building shakes. Something heavy rumbles up above. Dust trickles to the floor.

The dish is moving.

Someone wants him here now.

Shelbourne throws himself down the stairs, holding onto the railing with one hand and his printer with the other.

\---

There’s a light at the end of the hallway, illuminating a single door. It’s locked.

“Hillside\! Open up\!” he yells.

No one answers, but Shelbourne hears the sound of a heavy cabinet being dragged across the floor. He shoves at the door, putting his whole body into it. Forget the gun—Headquarters ought to have given him a crowbar. He finally breaks down the door—he’s always wanted to do that—and comes face to face with the sorry little man he’s been tailing for three months.

“Get back\!” Hillside shouts, his voice verging on manic.

Hillside’s eyes are wide and twitching. He’s holding one of his gadgets in his hand, and behind him are a bank of computers, their lights flickering on and off. Shelbourne shoves the man aside and looks for an off switch.

“It’s too late\!” Hillside shouts.

Shelbourne starts pressing buttons at random. Nothing seems to happen.

“What are you doing?”

“What are you doing\!?” Shelbourne snaps back. “Are you sending a signal?”

Hillside laughs. “It’s not a transmitter, you fool. It’s a receiver.”

A series of glowing nixie tubes display a list of numbers—the same as the code for the loading dock.

“Receiving what? Are you working for the Americans?”

“There aren’t any Americans on the moon anymore. But something else is\!”

Hillside glances at Shelbourne’s hand. Expecting to see a gun? Disappointed not to? No—a flicker of recognition at the printer.

That’s the gadget Hillside’s been tinkering with all this time.

Both printers buzz and snip at the same time. Shelbourne reads his slip of paper.

It says to kill him.

Agent Shelbourne sat in the cold, dark interior of his state-provided car, watching his assignment through her second floor apartment window. It was late autumn, 1974, and it was going to be a particularly cold and bitter winter. Right about now, Shelbourne’s warm bed was tempting.

Shelbourne had been tailing Hillside for several months now, and had come to the conclusion that this temporary reassignment was almost certainly a demotion due to an “unfortunate lack of discipline.” The State hadn’t even issued him a gun; instead, sat on the passenger seat beside him was his printer. Less of a gadget to help with his investigation, more of a leash: the hand-held device powered by a small radioactive isotope regularly spat out orders on an endless ribbon of tape, like a portable stock ticker. Shelbourne was jotting a few final notes on his clipboard when the thing buzzed and snipped a tiny slip of paper onto the seat. It read, “Alignment Required.”

With a sigh, Shelbourne finished his daily report, gathered his printer and his resolve, then stepped out into the biting cold. Just around the corner was the dead-drop location where he left his reports to be collected, probably by another nameless low-ranking agent.

If there was ever proof that his posting was meaningless, it was the stack of daily reports in the locked box, uncollected and growing damp. He couldn’t blame headquarters: each of the reports was mostly identical to the others. Professor Hillside was a woman of routine, not a woman of mystery. Right now, she would be finishing her dinner and settling in to listen to the radio and tinker with one of her many gadgets for a few hours before heading off to bed around midnight. The same as every other night. If she was a Western spy, Shelbourne couldn’t tell.

Professor Hillside wasn’t an administrator, or a head researcher, or even particularly well known on campus. What a waste of a career. For both of them: Shelbourne could be escorting diplomats or uncovering espionage against the State; instead he stalks this woman like a pervert, following her to and from the observatory, watching her eat from his car, and recording when she goes to bed.

When he returned to his car, he noticed Hillside’s apartment light was off. Down the street, a tiny figure trudged towards the bus stop.

\---

The campus was closed this late at night. Agent Shelbourne left his car in the lot and kept to the shadows, following Hillside down the lane between the imposing concrete campus buildings. A thrill began to bloom in his chest as he passed the building with her lab, and the administration building. At the end of the lane is the giant dish, looming over the campus. It was invisible at night, a black silhouette swallowing the shifting grey forest behind it, with only a ring of blinking red lights around its rim.

Hillside shouldn’t have access to the observatory.

She approached the service entrance, then stopped to glance over her shoulder.

Shelbourne hid behind a corner, hoping she hadn’t spied him. After a few moments, he poked his head out, catching the door close behind her. He waited a few moments longer, in case she came back outside, then rushed as quietly as he could to the door.

It was locked with a glowing green keypad. He looked around. No windows at ground level he would break, and no fire escape he could reach. The smart option was to wait for her to exit, apprehend her, and take her back to the local party office for questioning. Instead he hunted around the loading dock and came back with a heavy wrench.

But just as he was preparing to break down the door, his printer buzzed and snipped a short message into his other palm: a string of twelve digits.

His heart raced. He whirled around, expecting to see another agent watching him, or maybe a security camera aimed at him. Nothing.

The code worked. The door unlocked with a beep. He slipped inside, quietly shutting the door behind him. The inside was dark and silent, windowless, cut off from the winter outside. As he felt his way around the room—maybe a storage room, or a loading dock?—he heard the roar of a diesel engine coming to life, and screech of metal on metal. The floor rumbled, and dust trickled down from the ceiling.

The observatory was moving.

On the other side of the room was a metal staircase leading up. Fuck caution. He hurled himself up the stairs as the building morphed and twisted above him. A door at the top with a light on inside, illuminating the gap on the floor. He threw himself at it, breaking it open—he’d always imagined doing so—and came face to face with the manic, harried face of Professor Hillside.

He’s never seen her so close before.

She looked like an alien—eyes wide, teeth bared, hollering and scrambling backwards against what looks like a control panel. Behind her was a computer bank and above that, on the wall, a string of twelve numbers.

He held up the printer and its latest message: the numbers matched.

“It’s too late\!” Hillside shouted. “You can’t stop it\!”

Shelbourne shoves her aside and started pressing buttons at random.

“What are you doing?” she shrieked, trying to pull him away.

“What are you doing\!?” Shelbourne snapped back. “Are you sending a signal?”

“It’s not a transmitter, you fool. It’s a receiver.”

“Receiving what? Are you working for the Americans?\!”

Her face twists into a snarling laugh. She had something in her hand. A gun?

His printer buzzed and snipped a message into his palm.

Hillside froze and watched him read the instruction:

“Kill him.”

The wrench was heavy in his hand. Of course. She was an enemy of the state, sending messages to the West. Too dangerous to be left alive. He’ll be a hero.

She glanced at the gadget in her hand, then shrieked and threw herself at him.

Shelbourne didn’t think, only reacted. The wrench hit her in the neck and knocked her to the concrete floor with an awful crack. Blood pooled beneath her head.

Shelbourne panted, trembling with adrenaline.

She had dropped her gadget. Not a gun after all.

The dish shudders to a stop.

His printer buzzed in his hand. Hers did too.

Both printed and snipped tiny messages. He read his:

“Alignment successful.”

## Attempt 3

Agent Shelbourne sat in the cold, dark interior of his state-issued car and watched his assignment through her second floor apartment window. It was late autumn, 1974, and it was going to be a bitter winter.

His assignment, Comrade Hillside, was a woman of routine, not of ambition. Seemingly immune to politics, the old woman spent her days hunched over a tiny desk in a sterile laboratory at the observatory, and spent her nights tinkering with gadgets in her apartment. Shelbourne suspected that this temporary reassignment was almost certainly a permanent demotion due to what they called an “appalling lapse in judgement.”

The State hadn’t even issued him a gun; instead, sat on the passenger seat beside him was his printer. Less of a tool to help with his investigation, more of a leash: the hand-held device regularly spat out cryptic and barely helpful instructions on an endless ribbon of tape, like a portable stock ticker. It was probably radioactive.

Shelbourne was jotting a few final notes on his clipboard when the thing buzzed and snipped a tiny slip of paper onto the seat. It read, “alignment required.”

With a sigh, Shelbourne finished his report and was about to step into the cold when he saw her standing in her window. It was bright in her room and he was in shadow; there was no way she could see him. But she could recognized the car, and his habits were as routine as hers, by now.

After a few moments, she left the window.

Shelbourne climbed out of his car and hurried across the street. Inside the alley was the dead-drop location where he left his reports to be collected. If there was ever proof that his posting was meaningless, it was the stack of daily reports in the locked box, uncollected and growing damp.

What a waste of a career: Agent Shelbourne could have been escorting foreign dignitaries or uncovering western spies. Instead he spent his nights stalking this woman like a pervert.

When he returned to his car, he noticed Hillside’s apartment light was off. Hillside wasn’t due to turn in for the night, yet.

And down the street, a tiny figure trudged towards the bus stop.

At the other side of town, atop a hill, stood the Radio Observatory. Hailed as a triumph of Soviet science and engineering, it had fallen into disrepair as funding had dried up and party priorities had shifted elsewhere. It made everyone in town a little odd, a little paranoid, like an alien overlord watching —and intruding into—every facet of a person’s life.

Shelbourne left his car and followed Hillside on foot. The old woman was hunched over in the cold, hurrying from the bus stop to her laboratory on campus. Every few moments she looked around, checking over her shoulder for pursuers. 

A rumble shook the ground.
